Calorie Balance & Weight Loss

How many calories should I eat to lose fat? ‚ñº

Short answer: A moderate deficit of 300-500 calories below your maintenance level, which typically results in 0.5-1 lb per week of fat loss.

How to calculate:

  • Determine your maintenance calories (roughly: bodyweight in lbs √ó 15-16 for moderate activity)
  • Subtract 300-500 from that number
  • Track intake for 2-3 weeks, adjust if progress stalls

Why not more aggressive? Larger deficits cause:

  • Greater muscle loss (even with training)
  • Worse adherence (excessive hunger)
  • More metabolic adaptation
  • No faster fat loss long-term due to non-adherence
üí° Pro tip: Consistency with a moderate deficit beats perfection with an aggressive one. It's the diet you'll actually follow that works.
Based on research from Eric Trexler (MASS) and Alan Aragon
Does calorie counting work? Do I have to do it forever? ‚ñº

Does it work? Yes‚Äîit's the most reliable way to ensure you're hitting your calorie target.

Do you need it forever? No, not necessarily. Use it as a learning tool:

  • Phase 1 (4-8 weeks): Track everything to learn portion sizes and calorie density of foods
  • Phase 2 (ongoing): Use hand portions, visual guides, or intuitive eating based on what you learned
  • Check-ins: Return to tracking if progress stalls or goals change

Alternative methods if tracking feels unsustainable:

  • Hand-based portions (fist-sized carbs, palm-sized protein)
  • Container/measuring cup portions
  • Intuitive eating (for experienced individuals)
Recommended by Jordan Syatt and evidence-based coaches for behavior change

Protein & Macronutrients

How much protein should I eat? ‚ñº

Recommendation: 1.6-2.2 grams per kilogram of body weight daily

In pounds: 0.7-1.0 grams per pound of body weight

Examples:

  • 150 lb person: 105-150g protein/day
  • 200 lb person: 140-200g protein/day
  • 250 lb person: 175-250g protein/day

Important notes:

  • Higher amounts (>2.2 g/kg) don't provide additional muscle-building benefits
  • During fat loss, adequate protein helps preserve muscle mass
  • Total daily protein matters more than timing of individual meals
  • Spreading across 4-5 meals is practical but not mandatory
üí° Bottom line: Getting "enough" protein is important. Going way above won't help‚Äîeffort is better spent on training and calories.
Based on meta-analyses from Eric Helms, Eric Trexler, and Alan Aragon
How many carbs should I eat? ‚ñº

Standard recommendation: 3-4 grams per kilogram of body weight daily (for gym-focused individuals)

Why this amount? Carbs fuel training performance, especially for higher-volume workouts.

Context matters:

  • High-volume training (bodybuilding, CrossFit): 4+ g/kg beneficial
  • Moderate training (3-4x/week, moderate volume): 3-4 g/kg
  • Low-volume strength training (powerlifting): 2-3 g/kg can work
  • During fat loss: May tolerate lower carbs (2-3 g/kg), but performance may suffer

Are low-carb diets bad? No‚Äîthey can work for fat loss. But:

  • They work through creating a calorie deficit, not metabolic advantage
  • Training performance often suffers
  • Most people feel better and perform better on adequate carbs
Based on recent muscle glycogen research from Eric Trexler and MASS
Do I need dietary fat? Can I eat a low-fat diet? ‚ñº

Minimum fat intake: 0.8-1.2 g/kg body weight (or 20-30% of total calories)

Why fat matters:

  • Hormone production: Testosterone, estrogen, and other hormones require dietary fat
  • Vitamin absorption: Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) need fat for absorption
  • Gallstone prevention: Very low fat (<7g/day) during extreme diets increases gallstone risk
  • Satiety: Fat helps you feel full longer

Research shows: Fat intake below 20% of calories associated with reduced testosterone in men and reduced estrogen in women.

Practical application: Aim for at least 50-60g of fat daily for most people; don't fear it.

Based on meta-analysis by Eric Trexler in MASS September 2023

Nutrition Timing & Meal Frequency

Do I need to eat within 30 minutes after working out? ‚ñº

Short answer: Not really. The "anabolic window" is largely a myth.

What research shows:

  • Muscle protein synthesis is elevated for several hours after training
  • Eating within 2 hours is fine; timing within 30 min doesn't matter
  • Total daily protein and calories matter FAR more than meal timing

Practical advice: Eat something convenient containing protein and carbs within a few hours. That's it.

Real benefit of post-workout eating: If you're hungry or prefer eating post-workout, it can help you hit your daily macros.

Confirmed by Schoenfeld et al. and covered in MASS Research Review
How many meals per day should I eat? ‚ñº

The honest answer: It doesn't matter much. Pick what you prefer.

What matters instead:

  • Total daily calories and protein
  • Adherence (eating pattern you can stick with)
  • Personal preference and hunger patterns

Common approaches:

  • 3 meals/day: Traditional, works well for most
  • 4-5 meals/day: Better for high protein intake; allows smaller portions
  • Intermittent fasting (1-2 meals/day): Works for adherence in some people; no metabolic advantage

Bottom line: Use meal frequency to help you hit your targets and feel good. Change it only if current approach isn't working.

Should I try intermittent fasting? ‚ñº

Does it work for fat loss? Yes‚ÄîIF you create a calorie deficit. It has no metabolic advantage.

Potential benefits:

  • Easier to hit calorie targets (fewer eating windows)
  • May reduce hunger for some people
  • Simplifies meal planning

Potential downsides:

  • May increase hunger for others
  • Can negatively impact training performance (harder to fuel properly)
  • Makes hitting adequate protein more challenging
  • Doesn't preserve muscle during fat loss better than normal eating

Bottom line: Try it if you think you'd prefer it. If you don't like it, normal eating patterns work equally well.

Based on research from Alan Aragon and MASS

Food Choices & Diet Quality

Can I eat "junk food" and still get results? ‚ñº

For fat loss: Yes, if calories and protein are adequate.

What research shows:

  • Body composition is determined by calories and protein, not food quality
  • A calorie is a calorie for weight change (though not for other health markers)
  • You can lose fat eating processed foods if you maintain proper calories

BUT‚Äîwhole foods are superior for:

  • Satiety: Whole foods keep you fuller longer (easier to stick to diet)
  • Health: Better for blood lipids, blood sugar, micronutrient status
  • Energy: Better sustained energy throughout day
  • Adherence: Consistent diet quality makes adherence easier

The balanced approach: Eat ~80% whole, nutrient-dense foods; 20% flexible for enjoyment and adherence.

Recommended by Jordan Syatt, Andrew Coates, and evidence-based coaches

Progressive Overload & Muscle Growth

What's the most important thing for building muscle? ‚ñº

Progressive overload‚Äîgradually increasing training stress over time.

What this means: Incrementally add weight, reps, sets, or training frequency to challenge your muscles.

Examples:

  • Bench press: 185 lbs √ó 5 this week, 190 lbs √ó 5 next week
  • Leg press: 8 reps √ó 3 sets this week, 8 reps √ó 4 sets next week
  • Training frequency: squats 1x/week, progress to 2x/week

How fast should you progress?

  • Beginners: Can progress weekly (5-10 lb increases possible)
  • Intermediate: Progress every 2-3 weeks (2-5 lb increases realistic)
  • Advanced: Progress monthly or slower (1-2% increases)

Without progressive overload, no muscle growth occurs. This is the foundation.

Based on research from Brad Schoenfeld and supported by MASS
How many sets and reps should I do? ‚ñº

For muscle growth: 6-25+ sets per muscle group per week, with rep ranges from 5-30 reps.

More detailed breakdown:

  • Beginners: 6-10 sets per muscle per session √ó 1-2x/week
  • Intermediate: 10-15 sets per muscle per session √ó 2x/week
  • Advanced: 15-25+ sets per muscle per session √ó 2-3x/week

Rep range recommendations:

  • Heavy (3-6 reps): Targets strength and Type II fibers
  • Moderate (6-12 reps): Best for hypertrophy; sweet spot for most
  • High (12+ reps): Great for isolation; requires less recovery

Key finding: Muscle growth occurs across ALL rep ranges if volume is adequate.

Practical approach: Include variety (mix 6-8 reps, 8-12 reps, 12+ reps per muscle group).

Based on dose-response research from Schoenfeld et al.

Training Frequency & Recovery

Should I train each muscle once or twice per week? ‚ñº

Quick answer: Twice per week is slightly better, but once per week with adequate volume works.

What research shows:

  • Training muscle 2x/week produces ~10-15% more growth than 1x/week
  • The benefit decreases with higher frequency (3x/week offers only 5-10% additional benefit over 2x)
  • Total weekly volume matters more than frequency

Practical implications:

  • Beginners: 1x per week (full-body 3x/week) is fine for starting
  • Intermediate: 2x per week (upper-lower split) is ideal
  • Advanced: 2-3x per week (body part split or push-pull-legs)

Bottom line: Use frequency to help you accumulate adequate volume without excessive fatigue.

Research from Schoenfeld et al. covered in MASS
How much rest do I need between workouts? ‚ñº

Between sets:

  • Heavy compound (6 reps or less): 3-5 minutes
  • Moderate (6-12 reps): 2-3 minutes
  • Light isolation (12+ reps): 60-90 seconds

Between training sessions for same muscle: 48-72 hours minimum

Sleep: 7-9 hours nightly for optimal recovery

What research shows about recovery modalities:

  • Active recovery (light walking, easy cycling): Minimal benefit
  • Foam rolling: Minimal benefit for muscle recovery (though good for mobility)
  • Stretching: Limited benefit; focus on strength training instead

Bottom line: Most "recovery tools" don't matter much. Sleep and adequate calories matter more.

Based on reviews from Michael Zourdos in MASS
What is a deload and when should I take one? ‚ñº

What is a deload? A planned week of reduced training volume (typically 40-60% less sets) while maintaining weight/intensity.

When to take one:

  • Every 4-6 weeks of hard training
  • When strength plateaus or decreases
  • When experiencing excessive fatigue or joint pain
  • When motivation drops significantly

What happens during deload:

  • Nervous system recovers
  • Joint stress reduces
  • Inflammation decreases
  • You're refreshed for next training block

Research shows: Deloading prevents overtraining and enables continued progress; even improved progress in following weeks.

Evidence supports deloading for long-term progress (MASS and Alan Aragon)

Form & Exercise Selection

Does form need to be perfect to build muscle? ‚ñº

No‚Äîgood-enough form with progressive overload beats perfect form with no progress.

What matters for form:

  • Moving through a reasonable range of motion
  • Controlling the weight (not bouncing or using momentum)
  • Feeling tension in the target muscle

Common form mistakes to avoid:

  • Using weight too heavy (momentum does work, not muscle)
  • Reduced range of motion significantly
  • Bouncing reps (removes tension)
  • Rapid, uncontrolled reps

Reality: Most people benefit from slightly lighter weight with controlled reps than heavy weight with poor form.

Bottom line: Start with weight you can control; focus on movement quality and progressive overload.

Is full range of motion necessary? ‚ñº

For most people: Yes. Full ROM produces better results.

What research shows:

  • Full range of motion produces more hypertrophy than partial ROM
  • Produces greater strength gains
  • Better for long-term joint health (improves mobility)

Exceptions:

  • Joint pain: Stop before pain occurs
  • Injury/mobility limitations: Work within safe range
  • Advanced technique (partials) as supplement to full ROM training

Bottom line: Aim for full ROM when trained and stable; don't force it if you have mobility issues.

Based on ROM research supporting full movement patterns

Training to Failure

Should I train to failure every set? ‚ñº

For heavy compounds: No. For isolation: Can work but unnecessary.

What research shows:

  • Training to failure produces hypertrophy
  • Training 2-3 reps short of failure produces equal hypertrophy if volume is matched
  • Failure in heavy compounds increases injury risk without additional benefit

Practical approach:

  • Heavy compounds (squats, deadlifts, bench): Stop 2-3 reps short of failure
  • Moderate compounds (dumbbell press, rows): Can go to failure on final set
  • Isolation exercises: Can train closer to failure regularly

Using RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion):

  • RPE 7-8 (2-3 reps short of failure): Safe for all
  • RPE 9-10 (0-1 reps short/at failure): For final sets of isolation work

Bottom line: Adequate training stress (not necessarily failure) is what matters.

Research from Schoenfeld and confirmed by MASS

Sleep & Recovery

How much sleep do I need? ‚ñº

Optimal: 7-9 hours per night.

How sleep impacts training:

  • Less than 6 hours: 15-20% reduction in strength gain and muscle growth potential
  • 7-9 hours: Optimal recovery and adaptation
  • More than 9 hours: Diminishing returns

Why sleep matters:

  • Muscle protein synthesis (building muscle)
  • Growth hormone release
  • Testosterone production (men and women)
  • Glycogen replenishment
  • Mental health and mood

Tips for better sleep:

  • Consistent sleep schedule (same bedtime/wake time)
  • Dark, cool room (65-68¬∞F)
  • No screens 30-60 min before bed
  • Avoid caffeine after 2 PM
Based on research from Mah et al. and others

Hydration & Minerals

How much water should I drink daily? ‚ñº

General guideline: 35 ml per kilogram of body weight

Examples:

  • 150 lb person (68 kg): ~2.4 liters/day
  • 200 lb person (91 kg): ~3.2 liters/day

Adjustments for training:

  • Add 500 ml per hour of exercise
  • More in hot/humid environments
  • Individual sweat rates vary‚Äîtest yours

How to monitor hydration:

  • Urine color: Pale yellow = well hydrated; dark = dehydrated
  • Body weight: Shouldn't drop more than 2% during workout
  • Thirst: Drink when thirsty (though wait for very hard efforts)

During exercise:

  • Short efforts (<60 min): Water is sufficient
  • Longer efforts (>60 min): Add carbs (30-60g/hour) and sodium

Weight Tracking & Body Composition

Why does my weight fluctuate daily? ‚ñº

Weight fluctuates 3-5 lbs daily due to normal variations; only track trends.

Causes of daily fluctuation:

  • Water retention: Carbs, sodium, hormones, training all affect water
  • Digestion: Food in stomach/intestines weighs something
  • Hormones: Menstrual cycle can increase water retention 3-5 lbs
  • Training: Muscle damage causes inflammation and water retention

How to track properly:

  • Weigh yourself consistently (same time of day, preferably morning)
  • Track weekly averages, not daily weights
  • Expect weight to fluctuate; this is normal
  • Use multiple metrics: weight, photos, measurements, performance

Example: 0.5-1 lb weight loss per week is healthy fat loss; expect 3-5 lb daily swings.

Clarified by Eric Trexler in MASS

Evidence-Based Supplements

Which supplements actually work? ‚ñº

Tier 1: Highly Effective & Cost-Efficient

  • Creatine Monohydrate: 5-10% improvement in strength and hypertrophy; $10/month
  • Protein Powder: Convenient protein source; no special benefit vs. whole food
  • Vitamin D: If deficient; supports bone health, mood, immunity

Tier 2: Modest Benefits, Context-Dependent

  • Caffeine: 2-5% performance improvement; $5/month; timing matters
  • Citrulline Malate: Modest pump/endurance benefit; 6-9g pre-workout
  • Beta-Alanine: Benefits high-rep/repeated efforts; requires loading

Tier 3: Minimal/Inconsistent Benefits

  • Multi-ingredient pre-workout stacks (overlapping ingredients)
  • Fat burners (minimal effect on body composition)
  • BCAAs (unnecessary if adequate protein consumed)
  • Most other "trending" supplements

Bottom line: Creatine, protein, vitamin D, and caffeine cover 95% of supplement benefits. Everything else is marginal.

Based on systematic reviews from Eric Trexler in MASS
Is creatine safe? Does it cause side effects? ‚ñº

Creatine monohydrate is extremely safe; decades of research support this.

How it works:

  • Increases phosphocreatine stores in muscles
  • Improves ATP regeneration during intense efforts
  • Leads to ~5-10% improvement in strength and hypertrophy

Dosing:

  • Simple: 5g per day, every day (no loading needed)
  • Optional loading (faster saturation): 20g/day for 5-7 days, then 5g/day

Potential side effects (rare):

  • Mild water retention (1-2 lbs)
  • Digestive upset (use monohydrate, not other forms)

Does NOT cause: Kidney damage, liver damage, dehydration (in healthy people)

Cost: ~$10/month (incredibly cheap)

Supported by International Society of Sports Nutrition
Should I take protein powder or get protein from food? ‚ñº

Either works; choose based on convenience and cost.

Research shows: Whey protein, whole food protein, plant protein‚Äîall produce similar muscle gains if total protein is adequate.

When protein powder is useful:

  • Hard to hit protein targets with food alone
  • Cost-effective (cheaper than many meat sources)
  • Convenient (quick shakes)
  • Dietary restrictions (lactose, vegan)

When food is better:

  • Whole foods provide vitamins, minerals, fiber
  • More satiating (helps with adherence)
  • Less processed

Practical approach: Use 1-2 servings of protein powder if convenient; get rest from food. Total protein matters, not the source.

Supported by research comparing protein sources
What about vitamin D supplementation? ‚ñº

Supplementation recommended if deficient (most people, especially in winter).

Vitamin D benefits:

  • Bone health and calcium absorption
  • Immune function
  • Mood and mental health
  • Muscle function

Dosing:

  • If deficient: 2000-4000 IU/day
  • If adequate: 1000-2000 IU/day maintenance
  • Get levels tested; know where you stand

Sources:

  • Sunlight (15-30 min daily in summer months)
  • Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines)
  • Fortified milk/dairy
  • Supplements (if insufficient)

Cost: Very cheap (~$10-15/year)

Multi-Vitamins & Micronutrients

Do I need a multivitamin? ‚ñº

For most people eating varied diet: No. For those with gaps: Maybe.

Research shows: Multivitamins show minimal benefit for healthy individuals eating adequate diets.

When they might help:

  • Restrictive diets (very low calories, limited food variety)
  • Specific deficiencies (confirmed by testing)
  • Dietary restrictions (vegan: B12, iron; athlete: iron, calcium)

Better approach:

  • Assess diet for obvious gaps
  • Get blood work if concerned
  • Supplement only what's deficient
  • Prioritize whole foods for micronutrients

Bottom line: Focus on food first; supplement gaps individually.

Based on meta-analyses showing limited benefit of multivitamins

Calorie Balance: The Foundation

Why is calorie balance the most important factor? ‚ñº

The Core Principle: Total energy intake versus total energy expenditure determines changes in body weight. No diet works if calories aren't appropriate for your goal, regardless of macro composition.

Practical Application: Use the Mifflin-St Jeor equation with an activity multiplier to estimate maintenance calories, then adjust based on 2-week progress.

For Fat Loss: Create a 300-500 calorie deficit for 0.5-1 lb/week loss. Avoid aggressive deficits (>25 cal/kg fat-free mass) due to muscle loss and adherence issues.

For Muscle Gain: Use a 300-500 calorie surplus. Larger surpluses don't mean more muscle‚Äîexcess stores as fat.

Based on meta-analyses from Eric Trexler (MASS) and Alan Aragon

Macronutrient Guidelines

What are the optimal macronutrient ratios? ‚ñº

Protein: 1.6-2.2 g/kg body weight daily - Critical for muscle preservation during fat loss and muscle building during surplus.

Carbohydrates: 3-4 g/kg for gym-focused individuals - Fuel training performance; not mandatory for fat loss but most people perform better on adequate carbs.

Fat: 0.8-1.2 g/kg (minimum 20-30% of calories) - Essential for hormone production, vitamin absorption, and overall health.

Key Finding: The ratio matters far less than total calories and hitting minimum protein. Flexibility with carbs/fat is fine.

Based on dose-response research from Eric Helms and Schoenfeld et al.

Micronutrients & Supplementation

Which micronutrients should clients prioritize? ‚ñº

Whole food sources are superior to supplements for most micronutrients because they provide vitamins, minerals, and fiber together.

Key micronutrients:

  • Iron: Red meat, spinach, legumes (8-18mg/day)
  • Calcium: Dairy, leafy greens (1000-1200mg/day)
  • Magnesium: Nuts, seeds, dark chocolate (310-420mg/day)
  • Vitamin D: Sunlight, fatty fish, supplements if deficient (600-4000 IU/day)

Supplementation guidance: Multivitamins show minimal benefit for healthy people eating varied diets. Supplement only identified deficiencies.

Based on meta-analyses from MASS and nutrition research

Training Principles Foundation

What are the fundamental principles of muscle growth? ‚ñº

Three factors drive training adaptations (in order of importance):

  • Mechanical Tension: Force production and progressive overload‚Äîprimary driver
  • Muscle Damage: Disruption from challenging workouts
  • Metabolic Stress: Metabolite accumulation (pump, burn)

Critical insight: Total volume and progressive overload matter more than any specific technique.

Progressive overload strategies: Increase weight, reps, sets, training frequency, or density over time. Without progressive overload, no adaptation occurs.

Based on research from Brad Schoenfeld and supported by MASS

Volume & Hypertrophy

How much volume is needed for muscle growth? ‚ñº

Volume = Sets √ó Reps √ó Load - This is the most important variable for hypertrophy.

Research-backed recommendations:

  • Beginners: 6-10 sets per muscle per session
  • Intermediate: 10-15 sets per muscle per session
  • Advanced: 15-25+ sets per muscle per session

Rep range flexibility: Hypertrophy occurs across 3-30 reps if volume is adequate. Mix rep ranges for optimal results.

Practical application: Include strength (3-6 reps), moderate (6-12 reps), and higher rep (12+ reps) work per muscle group weekly.

Based on dose-response research from Schoenfeld et al. and covered in MASS

Training Frequency & Programming

How often should muscles be trained per week? ‚ñº

Research shows: 2x per week is slightly better than 1x, but 1x with adequate volume works.

Key findings:

  • Training 2x/week produces ~10-15% more growth than 1x/week
  • 3x/week offers only 5-10% additional benefit over 2x
  • Total weekly volume matters more than frequency

Program recommendations:

  • Beginners: 1x per week (full-body 3x/week)
  • Intermediate: 2x per week (upper-lower split)
  • Advanced: 2-3x per week (body part split or PPL)
Research from Schoenfeld et al. covered in MASS
